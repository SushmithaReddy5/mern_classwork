It was constructed in 1591 by Sultan Muhammad Quli Qutb Shah, the fifth ruler of the Qutb Shahi dynasty. The monument was built to commemorate the founding of Hyderabad and is believed to mark the end of a deadly plague that had devastated the region. The name "Charminar" is derived from two Urdu words — "Char" meaning four and "Minar" meaning towers or minarets. True to its name, the structure has four grand minarets, one on each corner, each standing approximately 56 meters (184 feet) tall. Charminar is an outstanding example of Indo-Islamic architecture with elements of Persian design. Built using granite, limestone, and mortar, it features a large central arch flanked by the four minarets. On the upper floor, there is a functioning mosque that continues to be a place of worship. Surrounded by vibrant markets like Laad Bazaar, and close to landmarks such as Mecca Masjid, the Charminar is not only an architectural masterpiece but also a bustling center of local culture, commerce, and tourism.
